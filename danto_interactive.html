The Reading

Arthur Danto was an American philosopher and art critic born in 1924. He spent most of his career at Columbia University in New York, where he became one of the most important thinkers about what art is and how we understand it. In 1964, he published a short but revolutionary essay called The Artworld, which changed the way philosophers, critics, and artists thought about art forever. Danto was not just a theorist; he also wrote weekly art criticism for The Nation magazine for over twenty years, meaning he was constantly looking at real art and asking real questions about it.

Danto's central question was surprisingly simple: why is one object art and another object not, when they look exactly the same? He was inspired by Andy Warhol's Brillo Boxes, sculptures that looked completely identical to the cardboard boxes found in supermarket stockrooms. If you put both boxes in a room, you could not tell them apart by looking. Yet one was worth thousands of dollars and displayed in a gallery, while the other was thrown away. Danto asked: what is the difference? His answer was not about appearance. It was about context.

Danto's answer was the concept of the Artworld. He argued that what makes something art is not its physical qualities, not how it looks, feels, or is made, but whether it is embedded within what he called an atmosphere of artistic theory. The Artworld is the network of people, institutions, and ideas that collectively define what counts as art at any given time. This includes galleries, museums, art critics, auction houses, curators, collectors, and art historians. When the Artworld accepts something as art, it becomes art. Without that acceptance, it remains just an object.

"To see something as art requires something the eye cannot detect, an atmosphere of artistic theory, a knowledge of the history of art."
Danto (1964, p. 580)

Before Danto, most people assumed that you could tell if something was art by looking at it, by its beauty, skill, or craftsmanship. Danto completely overturned this. He said that aesthetic qualities are irrelevant to whether something is art or not. A dirty bed, an empty canvas, a pile of bricks: these could all be art, not because of what they look like, but because of the context in which they exist. This was a profound shift. It meant that art could be anything, as long as the right people and institutions framed it as art. It also meant that the same object could be art in one setting and not art in another.

Tracey Emin's My Bed (1998) is a perfect example of Danto's theory in action. The bed, with its stained sheets, empty vodka bottles, used tissues, and cigarette packets, existed first in Emin's actual bedroom in London, where she spent several days unable to leave following a personal breakdown. In that context, it was not art. It was a bed. But when Emin moved it into the White Cube gallery and later to Tate Britain, the Artworld did its work. Critics wrote about it. The Turner Prize shortlisted it in 1999. In 2014, Christie's auction house sold it for £2,546,500. The Artworld had spoken. The object had not changed at all, but its status had changed completely.

Danto's theory has been both hugely influential and heavily criticised. Some philosophers, particularly George Dickie, developed his ideas further into what became known as the Institutional Theory of Art. Others argued that Danto gave too much power to elite institutions, effectively allowing a small group of wealthy and well-connected people to decide what counts as culture for everyone. If the Artworld is controlled by galleries and auction houses in New York and London, whose art gets excluded? Despite these critiques, Danto's core insight, that art is defined by context, not appearance, remains one of the most important ideas in the philosophy of art, and his influence can be felt in almost every contemporary debate about what art is.
